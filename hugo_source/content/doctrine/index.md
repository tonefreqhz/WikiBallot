## Odious Debt

Debts incurred by a regime without the consent of the people, not for the benefit of the people, and where the creditor knew this, are not binding on the people. The doctrine was applied in 1898 when the United States repudiated Cuba's debt to Spain. It applies now. It applies generally.

**Three conditions — all met:**

1. Incurred without consent — no parliament voted for the bank bailouts before they happened
2. Not for the benefit of the people — beneficiaries were institutional creditors, not depositors or workers
3. The creditor knew — BIS, Bank of England, Federal Reserve, and IMF all published warnings before 2008 and designed the bailouts anyway

## Taxes for Revenue Are Obsolete

Beardsley Ruml, Chairman of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, published this in *American Affairs*, 1946. A government that issues its own currency does not need tax revenue to fund spending. Taxes serve other purposes — controlling inflation, redistributing income, pricing externalities — but revenue is not one of them. The constraint on government spending is real: the productive capacity of the economy, the availability of labour and materials. The constraint is not financial.

The implication: every time a government says it cannot afford hospitals, schools, or housing, it is not making a statement about money. It is making a statement about power — about whose interests it serves.

## Monetary Sovereignty

A monetary sovereign is a government that issues its own currency, allows it to float on foreign exchange markets, and has no debts denominated in a foreign currency. The United Kingdom, the United States, Japan, Canada, and Australia are monetary sovereigns. Greece, under the euro, is not.

A monetary sovereign cannot run out of its own currency. It can always service debts denominated in that currency. The constraint is inflation — too much money chasing too few goods. The correct instrument for controlling inflation is taxation (which withdraws money from circulation) and interest rates, not the refusal to fund public services.

The Bank of England confirmed in its March 2014 Quarterly Bulletin that banks create money when they make loans — it does not come from deposits. Central banks create money when they purchase assets. The money supply is not fixed. It is a policy variable.

## The Check-Weighman Principle

The Coal Mines Regulation Act 1860 established the right of miners to appoint their own check-weighman — an independent verifier who counted the coal at the pithead alongside the colliery's own weighman. Before this, miners were paid on the colliery's figures alone. The check-weighman made the measurement accountable.

The principle: every figure presented by an institution that benefits from that figure must be independently verified.
